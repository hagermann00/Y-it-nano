**What to Keep:**
- Name + 1-line archetype
- Why they tried
- Core failure mechanism
- Financial impact
- Key lesson

**What to Cut:**
- Month-by-month breakdown (consolidate to "Timeline to failure: X months")
- Detailed plan explanation (1 sentence summary)
- Elaborate on opportunity cost

## ANONYMIZATION RULES

### Required Changes
- ✅ Change all real names (use composite first names)
- ✅ Change specific companies/platforms (use "a major platform" or generic)
- ✅ Change locations (use region only: "West Coast", "Midwest")
- ✅ Change timestamps (use "2023-2024" instead of specific dates)
- ✅ Change identifying details (job titles generalized)
- ✅ Change financial specifics (round to nearest $500)

### Allowed Details
- ✅ Age ranges (±5 years)
- ✅ General background (e.g., "software engineer")
- ✅ Emotional journey (the failure is the point)
- ✅ Realistic timelines (failure typically 4-9 months)
- ✅ Failure mechanisms (the core insight)

## CASE STUDY EXAMPLES

### EXAMPLE 1: AMAZON FBA

**Full Version (1,200 words - Internal Use)**

```
# Case Study 003: Marcus - The Technical Expert

## Character Profile
- Name: Marcus (composite)
- Age: 34
- Background: Software engineer with 8 years AWS experience
- Why He Tried: "My tech skills give me an edge. If everyone else fails,
  I can optimize my way to success."
- Starting Capital: $8,000
- Available Time: 40-50 hours/week (quit job to do this full-time)

## The Plan
Marcus spent 2 months researching products using Jungle Scout and Helium 10.
He found a "low-competition" niche: smart home cable organizers.
Demand: 2,500 monthly searches. Competition: 47 listings (he thought this was low).

He ordered 1,000 units from a manufacturer at $3.50 each, shipping to Amazon FBA.
Total outlay: $3,500 for inventory + $800 for shipping to FBA + $200 for barcode/UPC.
His plan: Sell at $14.99, achieve 25% margin = $11.49 net.
Expected break-even: 150 units (roughly 2 months at 75 units/month).

## Month 1-2: Reality Check
- Amazon received inventory after 35 days
- First week: 8 sales
- By end of month: 28 sales total
- Reality fee hit: Referral (15%) + FBA fulfillment ($1.84/unit) + storage ($0.15/unit)
  = $5.14 in fees per sale
- Actual margin: $14.99 - $3.50 (COGS) - $5.14 (fees) = $6.35 (42% margin, not 25%)
- Problem: His calculator was wrong

Wait, this is actually better than expected. But then...

## Month 3-4: The Saturation Shock
- New competitors launched (he checked Jungle Scout rankings)
- By week 3 of month 3: 47 competitors became 61 competitors
- Buy Box battle: Prices dropped from $14.99 to $12.99, then $10.99
- His sales: Peaked at 35 units (month 2) then dropped to 18 units (month 3)
- At $10.99, his margin is now: $10.99 - $3.50 - $1.50 (new fee reality) = $5.99
- The repricing wars are destroying his profit

## Month 5-6: The Storage Fee Trap
- He still has 650 units unsold
- Monthly storage fees: $0.75/cubic foot × 80 cubic feet = $60/month
- But wait... Amazon calculates storage on units in inventory
- For 650 units taking up 80 cubic feet: Effective cost = $0.12/unit/month
- With price war margins at $5-6/unit, he's now breaking even or losing money

He tries to accelerate sales with Facebook ads: $500/month budget
- Spends $1,000 on ads (months 5-6)
- Gets 40 additional sales from ads
- But at current prices, profit per sale = $3-4
- Ad cost per acquisition: $25
- He's losing money on every ad sale

## Month 7: The Policy Shock
- Amazon announces new capacity restrictions
- He gets a notice: His inventory account is "over-utilized"
- New rules: Can only have 400 units in FBA per month based on velocity
- His 650 units trigger overage fees: $0.50/unit/month on excess

He panics and tries to liquidate:
- Offers units to liquidation company: Get $4/unit (losing money per unit)
- Sells 200 units through liquidation for $800
- Still has 450 units in FBA warehouse

## Month 9-12: Slow Death
- His stored inventory now costs him $150-200/month in fees
- Sales have collapsed to 5-8/month
- He's hemorrhaging $100-150/month just keeping inventory in FBA
- After 9 months, he's lost $3,200+ out of $8,000 initial capital
- Time invested: 400+ hours
- Hourly rate: -$8/hour (negative)

## Final Decision
Marcus shuts down the FBA account, liquidates remaining inventory at major loss,
and accepts the defeat. His "technical edge" was irrelevant. The fundamental problem:
He entered an increasingly saturated market with insufficient capital to weather
inventory cycles and fee shocks.

## Financial Summary
- Initial investment: $8,000
- Additional ad spend: $1,000
- Total capital deployed: $9,000
- Final loss: $5,200
- Time invested: 450 hours
- Effective hourly rate: -$11.55/hour

## The Real Problem
Marcus believed his technical skills would give him a competitive advantage.
In reality, product selection and market timing matter infinitely more than optimization.
He entered the market at the wrong time (when competition was rising),
selected a product with sufficient search volume to attract competitors,
and had insufficient capital to sustain through inventory cycles. No amount of
AWS knowledge could fix these structural problems.

## What He Wishes He Knew
1. "Market saturation accelerates faster than research shows. 47 competitors in
   week 1 becomes 61 by week 12."
2. "FBA fees are not fixed. New policies get introduced constantly that change
   your unit economics."
3. "Technical skills are irrelevant when you're competing on commodity products.
   Everyone has the same suppliers and tools."
4. "Initial capital of $8K is insufficient. You need 3-4x to survive typical
   failure cycles."
5. "Velocity matters more than total sales. Slow-moving inventory destroys
   profitability through fees, not revenue."

## Archetype
Technical Expert (Belief: "My edge will work if others fail")
Failure Mechanism: Market saturation + Fee policy shock + Capital insufficiency
```

```
## Case Study 3: Marcus - The Technical Expert

**Background:** Software engineer, 8 years AWS experience. Thought technical
skills would give him an unfair advantage in FBA.

**Why He Tried:** "If everyone else fails, I can optimize my way to success."

**What Happened:**
Marcus found a "low-competition" smart home product: 2,500 monthly searches,
47 competitors. He ordered 1,000 units at $3.50 cost, $8,000 total investment.

Month 1-2: Sales started at 28 units. Fees ate his margin (not 25% as planned,
but 42% due to Amazon's referral + fulfillment charges).

Month 3-4: Competition exploded to 61 sellers. Price war commenced: $14.99 →
$10.99. His margin collapsed to $5/unit at new prices.

Month 5-6: 650 units sitting in FBA warehouse. Storage fees piling up. He spent
$1,000 on Facebook ads to accelerate sales. At $25 customer acquisition cost,
each ad sale lost him $15.

Month 7: Amazon's capacity restrictions hit. His inventory flagged as
"over-utilized." New fees: $0.50/unit/month on excess. He liquidated 200 units
at $4 each (major loss).

Month 9-12: Remaining 450 units cost $150-200/month in storage alone. Sales
collapsed to 5-8/month. He was bleeding $100-150/month just to keep inventory
stored. After 9 months, he'd lost $5,200 of his $8,000 investment and worked
450 hours (negative $11.55/hour).

**The Real Problem:**
Marcus's technical advantage was worthless in a commodity market. Product selection
and capital sufficiency mattered infinitely more than optimization skills. Market
saturation accelerates faster than research shows. Amazon policy changes destroy
margins unpredictably. And $8K capital is insufficient to weather typical failure cycles.

**Key Lesson:**
Technical skills don't solve fundamental market problems. When competing on commodity
products with shrinking margins and rising competition, your only advantage is capital
to absorb losses while you adapt. Marcus didn't have it.

---
**Archetype:** Technical Expert | **Failure Months:** 9 | **Total Loss:** -$5,200 + 450 hours
```

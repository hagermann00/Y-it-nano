# Case Study 005: Tyler Rodriguez

## Character Profile
- **Name:** Tyler Rodriguez (composite, anonymized)
- **Age:** 21
- **Background:** Junior at state university, business major
- **Income:** $0 (parental support $800/month + student loans)
- **Why They Tried:** Wanted to "build empire before graduation," avoid corporate job; influenced by campus entrepreneur club and TikTok dropshipping accounts showing "laptop lifestyle"

## Their Plan
- Chose trending products approach (whatever's viral on TikTok)
- Budget: $1,500 (summer job savings—supposed to cover textbooks and spring break)
- Time: 25-30 hours/week (flexible student schedule)
- Goal: $2,000/month by graduation to skip job recruiting, "be my own boss"
- Strategy: TikTok organic content + Facebook ads, ride trending product waves

## What Happened

### Month 1 - January 2024 (Spring Semester)
Week 1: Discovered dropshipping through TikTok "day in the life" influencer. Binge-watched 30 hours of content during winter break. Week 2-3: Setup Shopify, bought $97 "TikTok Dropshipping" course. Imported viral products: mini projector, portable blender, galaxy lamp. Week 4: Posted 12 TikToks showing products (used VPN to download from Chinese creators, re-uploaded). TikTok account banned for copyright violation after 3 days. Views before ban: 1,247 total across 12 videos, 3 store visits, 0 sales.

Switched to Facebook ads: $400 on mini projector. Results: 3 sales ($119.97 revenue). Felt successful until realized: Paid $133 per customer, selling at $39.99. Math didn't work. Net: -$380.

### Month 2 - February 2024
Midterms arrived—neglected studying for dropshipping. "This is my real education," he told roommates. Failed economics exam (ironic given dropshipping economics failure).

Tested 4 more trending products: LED mask, phone ring light, posture corrector, scalp massager. Ad spend: $600. Results: 8 sales, $278 revenue. Still bleeding money but "learning so much!"

Friend from entrepreneur club launched competing store—selling identical products from same AliExpress suppliers. Realized market saturated even within campus. Net: -$422.

### Month 3 - March 2024
Shiny object syndrome kicked in. Discovered new opportunity: Amazon FBA. Spent 40 hours researching, bought $297 "FBA Masterclass." Then discovered crypto trading. Spent 30 hours learning technical analysis, bought $197 crypto course.

Meanwhile, dropshipping store neglected. Spent final $400 on ads half-heartedly while focused on "next big thing." Results: 4 sales, $156 revenue. Customer complained about 40-day shipping—left negative review. Never responded (was busy learning crypto).

### Week 13 - Abandoned
Budget depleted: $1,500 summer savings gone. Spring break cancelled (couldn't afford). Textbooks purchased on credit card ($450—18% interest). Store abandoned—Shopify subscription kept charging for 2 months before Tyler remembered to cancel ($58 waste).

Total dropshipping results: 15 sales, $554 revenue over 13 weeks. Never returned to it. Moved to Amazon FBA attempt (failed after $800 loss), then crypto (lost $600), then NFTs (lost $300). Pattern: 3-month attention span per opportunity.

## Key Failure Mechanism

**Insufficient Budget + Shiny Object Syndrome + Lack of Commitment:**

Tyler represents the classic college student archetype: High enthusiasm, low follow-through. Attracted to "entrepreneur" identity more than building actual business. His $1,500 budget allowed testing only 3-4 products properly—far below 15-20 tests needed to find winner. Compounded by moving to next trend before completing full dropshipping test cycle.

**The Terrible Math:**
- Total ad spend: $1,400
- Total revenue: $554 (15 sales @ $36.93 average)
- COGS: $285 (products $220 + fees $65)
- Gross profit: $269
- Other costs: $645 (Shopify $145, courses $691 total across ventures, apps $60, abandoned subscription $58)
- **Net loss: -$1,776 (dropshipping portion only; -$3,276 including other failed ventures)**

**Why College Students Fail:**
1. **Insufficient capital:** $1,500 sounds like "a lot" to student but is 30-50% below minimum viable budget
2. **Opportunity cost blindness:** 200 hours @ $15/hour campus job = $3,000 PROFIT vs. -$1,800 loss
3. **Shiny object syndrome:** Young, curious, easily distracted by next opportunity before mastering current one
4. **No skin in the game:** Using "free money" (student loans, parental support) reduces commitment vs. hard-earned wages
5. **Identity over execution:** Wanted to "be entrepreneur" more than doing boring work of systematic testing, customer service, optimization

**The Pattern:**
Tyler's behavior mirrors 65% of student dropshippers: Try for 8-12 weeks → Don't achieve instant success → Move to next trend → Repeat. Never stay long enough to succeed at anything. The research shows 10-12% student success rate—but only for those who commit 6+ months.

## What They Wish They Knew

1. **"Laptop lifestyle" is performative marketing:** Those TikTok influencers earn money from affiliate links to Shopify, course sales, and brand deals—NOT dropshipping profit. Their actual business model is content creation, not e-commerce.

2. **Trending products are 30-day opportunities:** By the time a product is "trending on TikTok," 500+ dropshippers already launched it. The profit window closed 2-3 weeks before Tyler discovered it. First movers profit; followers lose.

3. **Jack of all trades, master of none:** Attempting dropshipping + FBA + crypto + NFTs in 9 months means zero mastery. Successful entrepreneurs pick ONE path, commit 12-24 months, master it. Tyler's diversification was procrastination disguised as strategy.

4. **Campus job beats speculative ventures:** $15/hour campus library job = $3,000 semester for 200 hours. Guaranteed, no risk, builds work ethic. His 200 hours lost $1,800 instead.

5. **Student loans are NOT investment capital:** Borrowed money for education used for dropshipping courses and ads. Will repay $2,000 of losses at 6.8% interest over 10 years = $2,720 total cost. Catastrophic financial decision at age 21.

## Post-Mortem Analysis

Tyler graduated with $34,000 student loan debt (including $2,000 from business attempts), 2.7 GPA (damaged by neglecting studies), zero business success. Status 2 years post-graduation:

**What the $1,800 could have bought instead:**
- Entire semester of textbooks (saved $800 in credit card interest)
- Spring break networking trip (career connections worth $10K+ salary)
- Professional wardrobe for job interviews
- SAT prep to enter competitive graduate program
- Reduced student loan burden by 5.3%

**What the 200 hours could have accomplished:**
- Campus job earning $3,000 (vs. losing $1,800)
- Internship applications leading to $60K post-grad offer (vs. $42K accepted)
- Academic focus improving GPA 2.7→3.2 (unlocks better opportunities)
- Skill development (coding, Excel, data analysis) for career
- Professional networking, informational interviews, mentorship

**Life Impact:**
- Student loan debt: $34,000 (including $2,000 from failed ventures)
- Credit card debt: $450 textbooks at 18% APR
- GPA damage: 2.7 (missed dean's list by 0.3, lost scholarship eligibility)
- Job search: Accepted $42K offer (vs. $55K for peers with 3.3+ GPA and internships)
- Opportunity cost over 10-year career: ~$150,000 in lower starting salary trajectory
- Psychological: Identified as "failed entrepreneur" instead of focusing on strengths

**What He Did Next:**
Worked $42K marketing coordinator role for 2 years. Realized he enjoyed marketing tactics (SEO, content, ads) but hated operational businesses. Pivoted to digital marketing agency role at $58K. By age 25, earned $72K as paid ads specialist—using Facebook ads skills learned from dropshipping, but applied to client work with guaranteed salary. Finally found product-market fit: His skill (ads) + market need (companies need ads) = sustainable career.

**Lesson Learned:**
"I was seduced by 'entrepreneur' identity without wanting to do entrepreneur work. I chased 4 different opportunities in 9 months—dropshipping, FBA, crypto, NFTs—because I wanted to 'strike it rich' and skip the boring corporate grind. Reality check: I never committed to anything long enough to succeed. The TikTok influencers I followed made money selling courses and affiliate links to people like me. THAT was their business.

Five years later, I make $72K as ads specialist—using the ONE useful skill I learned from dropshipping (Facebook ads), but applied properly: Helping companies who PAY me guaranteed salary instead of gambling on selling $20 products. I also have $26,000 in student loan debt still (down from $34,000)—will take another 8 years to pay off. That $2,000 I 'invested' in business ventures at age 21 is costing me $2,720 total. Most expensive 'education' I never finished."

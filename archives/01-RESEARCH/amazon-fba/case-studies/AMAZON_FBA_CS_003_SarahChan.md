# Case Study 003: Sarah Chan

## Character Profile
- **Name:** Sarah Chan (composite, anonymized)
- **Age:** 29
- **Background:** Software engineer, stable income
- **Income:** $120,000/year
- **Why They Tried:** "I have consistent income, so I can afford the startup costs. Should be able to handle this."

## Their Plan
- Sell fitness accessories (resistance bands, yoga mats, foam rollers)
- Budget: $3,500 from savings for initial inventory
- Timeline: 30-day manufacturing + 30-day shipping + 5-day FBA processing = 65 days capital in transit
- Personal cash reserves: $8,000 (should be enough)
- Goal: First batch returns profit in 60 days to fund second batch

## What Happened

### Days 1-65 (Capital Tied Up)
Sarah placed order for $3,500 inventory via supplier. Manufacturing: 30 days. Shipping: 35 days. FBA processing: 5 days. Total: 70 days before any sales possible. Her $3,500 was locked up for 2+ months.

### Day 70 (Surprise #1)
Received inventory. Found 15 units damaged in shipping (supplier disputed, Amazon inspection disqualified 18 more). Only 267 of 300 units approved for sale. Lost $200 in value immediately. Still profitable if sales were projected correctly.

### Days 71-105 (Sales Ramp)
Expected 40-60 units/month. Actual: 15 units week 1, 12 units week 2, 18 units week 3, 25 units week 4 = 70 units month 1. Slightly ahead! But...

### Day 90 (The Emergency)
Sarah's car needed transmission repair: $2,100. Her $8,000 emergency fund wasn't designed for this. With $3,500 still invested, effective liquid reserves were now $1,400. She had to use a credit card (18% APR) for the repair.

### Days 106-135
Now dealing with car payment + credit card minimum payment + startup cash flow misalignment. Sales came in at 65, 55, 48 units (declining per month as product aged without marketing). Revenue: $2,100. But net profit after fees: only $680. Not enough to cover car costs or credit card.

### Day 140+
By week 20, Sarah realized she couldn't scale (couldn't invest in second batch due to car debt). She'd be paying credit card interest (18% APR) on a car repair while her FBA capital was locked in slow-moving inventory. Decision: liquidate inventory at 45% loss. Total investment gone.

## Key Failure Mechanism

**Capital Trap During Working Capital Cycle:** Sarah's mistake wasn't poor business planning—it was underestimating the impact of capital lock-up duration (70 days) against an emergency fund only 2x the size of the investment. A single life emergency (medical, car, family) during the capital-in-transit window makes the business unrecoverable. She couldn't sustain both the debt and the FBA investment.

## What They Wish They Knew

1. **70-90 day capital lock-up is REAL:** Manufacturing (30 days) + shipping (30-60 days) + sales ramp (30 days) = 90-120 days before meaningful profit return
2. **Emergency fund needs to be 3-5x the investment:** She had 2.3x; needed 5x+
3. **One personal emergency + locked capital = business death:** No recourse
4. **Engineers think systematically but miss risk:** Didn't build in buffer for supply chain delays or personal emergencies

## The Math
- Initial investment: $3,500
- Damaged/loss inventory: $350
- Credit card interest (6 months): $315
- Liquidation loss: $1,680 (45% loss to clear inventory quickly)
- Total loss: **$3,845** (rounded to $3,800)
- Revenue generated: $1,950
- Effective hourly rate: -$27.14/hour

**Lesson:** "Having money isn't the same as having available capital. My savings looked healthy until my car broke. That's when I realized my capital was trapped in a business generating $12/hour returns while I was paying 18% APR on debt."

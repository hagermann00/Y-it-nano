# Case Study 006: David Lim

## Character Profile
- **Name:** David Lim (composite, anonymized)
- **Age:** 36
- **Background:** Operations manager, supply chain experience
- **Income:** $72,000/year
- **Why They Tried:** "My supply chain background gives me an edge. I can negotiate with suppliers."

## Their Plan
- Sell travel pillows and portable phone stands
- Source from Chinese manufacturer: $2.50/unit COGS
- Pricing: $14.99-19.99 range
- First batch: $2,500 for 1,000 units
- Margin expectation: 25%+ (supply chain efficiency)
- Timeline: "Scale to 5 SKUs by month 6"

## What Happened

### Months 1-2
Received first batch (1,000 units). Quality inspection: mostly good, 5% defect rate (not great but acceptable for startup). Listed products. Sales started: 80 units week 1. Customer feedback: positive reviews. David felt his supply chain experience was paying off.

### Month 2-3
Sales increased: 120 units week 1, 110 units week 2. But customer feedback shifted. Complaints:
- "Phone stand is wobbly"
- "Pillow lost shape after 1 week"
- "Cheap plastic, feels different than photos"
- Average rating dropping: started at 4.4 stars, dropped to 3.9 by week 3

### Month 3 Crisis
Reorder arrived: 2nd batch from same manufacturer. Quality inspection revealed: 22% defect rate. Handles loose, materials brittle, packaging damaged. Completely unacceptable.

David contacted manufacturer—delays, excuses, communication issues (time zones, language barriers). Meanwhile, his reviews tanked. Customer complaints arrived at rate of 15-20/day. Amazon seller rating dropped below 4.0 stars.

### Week 2 of Month 3: ACCOUNT SUSPENSION WARNING
Amazon flagged David's account: "High defect rate detected. Category approval at risk." 3-day notice to improve seller metrics or lose selling privileges in the category. This category was 60% of his revenue.

David had no recourse. The 2nd batch was stuck in FBA warehouse (damaged goods). He couldn't sell from it. Manufacturer denied liability. He was stuck with:
- 1st batch inventory: 700 unsold units (now with damaged reputation)
- 2nd batch inventory: 900 defective units (worthless)
- Account suspension threat: 3 days to resolve or lose selling privileges

### Month 4
David liquidated all inventory at 60% loss (sold to liquidation buyer). Attempted to restore seller metrics by removing damaged listings. Amazon account remained suspended from the category for 60 days.

Business couldn't survive the suspension. Remaining inventory ($1,400 value) sold for $550. Closed shop.

## Key Failure Mechanism

**Supplier Quality Collapse:** David's supply chain experience taught him to negotiate, track metrics, and optimize costs—but it didn't account for the randomness of Chinese manufacturing. 2nd batch failure (22% defect rate vs. 5%) wasn't operator error; it was supplier cutting corners mid-contract. One bad batch can trigger account suspension, loss of category approval, and liquidation necessity.

## What They Wish They Knew

1. **Chinese suppliers are uncontrollable for quality:** Even with contracts, 2nd batch can have 4x the defects of 1st batch
2. **Quality issues → review collapse → account suspension → business death:** This cascade happens fast (2-3 weeks)
3. **Category approval suspension is essentially a business shutdown:** Can't recover while suspended; can't generate revenue
4. **Supply chain expertise in retail ≠ supply chain expertise in FBA:** Low-volume, high-margin retail tolerates 5% defects. FBA doesn't

## The Math
- Revenue (3.5 months): $7,800
- COGS: $4,200
- FBA fees and referral: $1,500
- 1st batch liquidation loss: $700
- 2nd batch liquidation loss: $850
- Time (170 hours @ $20/hr opportunity): $3,400
- Total loss: **-$2,850** (rounded to $2,800)
- Effective hourly rate: **-$16.47/hour**

**Lesson:** "I thought my supply chain background was an asset. It wasn't. You can't manage supplier quality when manufacturing happens 7,000 miles away with zero recourse. One bad batch and Amazon suspends your account. That's not a business hiccup—that's a business death."

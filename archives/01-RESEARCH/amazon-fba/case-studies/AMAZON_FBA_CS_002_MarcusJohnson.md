# Case Study 002: Marcus Johnson

## Character Profile
- **Name:** Marcus Johnson (composite, anonymized)
- **Age:** 42
- **Background:** Former retail manager, 15 years business experience
- **Income:** $68,000/year
- **Why They Tried:** "I understand retail margins better than most," thought his experience would guarantee success

## Their Plan
- Sell electronics organizers and cable management systems
- Initial investment: $3,500 for 300 units
- Calculated margins: Product cost $4 → Amazon price $19.99 → "Profit: $10/unit" (50% margin assumed)
- Goal: 100 units/month = $1,000/month profit by month 3
- Timeline: "Break even in 3 months, then scale"

## What Happened

### Month 1
Launched 8 products. 45 units sold, $898 revenue. Marcus was optimistic. But when he reviewed the selling central settlement report:
- Referral fee (15%): -$134
- FBA fulfillment fee: -$112 (at $2.50/unit for 45 units)
- Monthly storage: -$18
- Actual net: $634 (vs. expected $450)

Actually profitable! He thought. But he was using incomplete data.

### Month 2
140 units sold (it grew!), $2,800 revenue. Marcus felt vindicated. But the real settlement:
- Referral fees: -$420
- FBA fulfillment: -$350
- Storage: -$45
- Returns processing (new June 2024 fee): -$80
- Damaged inventory reimbursement denials: -$65
- Net: $1,440 (vs. his calculation of $1,400—close!)

Marcus assumed he was on track.

### Month 3 (The Reckoning)
180 units sold. But:
- Referral fees: -$540
- FBA fulfillment: -$450
- Returns: -$85
- Storage (now building up): -$72
- Returns processing: -$108
- Amazon fee error correction (from earlier): -$45
- Net: $900

Marcus realized his margin was actually 32%, not the 50% he calculated. The "fine print" fees were 18% of revenue, not the 5% he budgeted. Over 6 months, his actual profit would be $800-1,000/month, not $1,000/month—a 20% shortfall.

### Month 4-6
Compounded discoveries: return rate was 12% (vs. 5% assumed). A product category he added got hit with 20% referral fees (not standard 15%). His competitive advantage eroded as prices dropped. By month 6, margins had compressed from 32% to 24%. Net income dropped to $600/month.

After 6 months of "successful" operations, Marcus realized he was earning $12/hour for the ongoing work. He liquidated inventory at 40% loss and closed shop after month 7.

## Key Failure Mechanism

**Fee Miscalculation + Margin Compression:** Marcus's "retail expertise" didn't translate to Amazon's complex fee structure. He systematically underestimated total fees by $200-300/month, or 20% of gross revenue. Add in competitive pressure and category-specific fee increases, his sustainable margin collapsed from 32% to 18%.

## What They Wish They Knew

1. **Amazon's settlement report has 12+ fee line items:** Can't calculate true margins from price alone
2. **Returns processing fees, category variation, and hidden charges:** Real COGS is 40-50% of selling price, not 30-35%
3. **Experience in traditional retail doesn't translate:** Inventory management, margin calculation, and pricing psychology are fundamentally different
4. **Competitive pressure erodes margins monthly:** In categories with >50 sellers, prices drop 5-10% quarterly

## The Math
- 6 months revenue: $8,100
- Actual net profit: $4,200
- Time invested: 220 hours
- Effective hourly rate: $19.09/hour
- But after liquidation loss: **-$4,200**
- Final effective rate: **-$19.09/hour**

**Lesson:** "Having retail experience made me overconfident. I thought I understood margins. I didn't account for Amazon's math being completely different from brick-and-mortar retail."

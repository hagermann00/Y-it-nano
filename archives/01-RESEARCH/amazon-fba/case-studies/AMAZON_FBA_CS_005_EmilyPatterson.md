# Case Study 005: Emily Patterson

## Character Profile
- **Name:** Emily Patterson (composite, anonymized)
- **Age:** 31
- **Background:** Product designer, worked for retail company
- **Income:** $70,000/year
- **Why They Tried:** "I understand product quality better than most sellers. My return rate should be lower."

## Their Plan
- Sell kitchen utensil sets (high-quality stainless steel)
- Initial investment: $3,200 for 200 units
- Margin calculation: COGS $8 → Amazon $24.99 → "Net $10/unit after all fees"
- Assumption: Return rate 5% (lower than market average of 10%)
- Projected profit: 200 units × $9.50/unit (conservative $10 minus 5% return loss) = $1,900

## What Happened

### Month 1-2
Launched 4 SKUs (product variants). 80 units sold over 2 months, $1,950 revenue. Emily's product quality seemed on point—minimal defect complaints. But...

### Month 3
Return notifications started arriving. Customers claiming:
- "Doesn't match product photos" (shipping damage not their fault)
- "Handle broke after 2 weeks" (1 unit)
- "Different from description" (color variance in batch)
- "Changed my mind" (Amazon's generous 30-day return policy)

Returns in Month 3: 14 units = 17.5% return rate (3.5x her assumption). She'd lose the product cost ($8 × 14 = $112) plus returns processing fees.

### Month 4
Sales increased: 95 units. But returns also spiked: 18 units = 19% return rate.

Emily's units were experiencing ~15-18% return rates, not the 5% she budgeted. Each returned unit cost her:
- Lost product: -$8
- Return shipping: -$3.50 (Amazon covers customer, Emily absorbs carrier fees)
- Restocking/processing: -$1.50
- Returns processing fee (new June 2024): -$0.75
- Total per return: -$13.75

Per returned $24.99 sale, she lost $13.75 = 55% of revenue gone.

### Month 5-6
Continued with 90-100 units/month but 15-16% return rates. Emily's total revenue $4,900 but returns ate $1,300 (26.5% of total revenue lost). Actual profit margin: 8% instead of the projected 38%.

She also discovered: 3 units marked "defective" in FBA when returned (quality issues she couldn't have prevented—supplier QC problem). Amazon didn't reimburse fully.

### Month 7
Emily did the math: She'd sold 520 units for $12,500 revenue. Lost to returns and fees: $3,400. Net income: $4,200 for 7 months = $600/month = $3.16/hour of work.

Remaining inventory: 80 units. Remaining working capital: zero. Couldn't afford to scale or fix QC issues. Closed shop.

## Key Failure Mechanism

**Return Rates Destroy Profitability:** Emily's return rate assumption (5%) was 3x too optimistic. Industry data for kitchen products averages 12-15%. Her actual rate: 15-18%. Combined with new returns processing fees and partial reimbursement denials, returns consumed 25-30% of gross revenue—making the business economically unviable.

## What They Wish They Knew

1. **Return rates vary wildly by product category:** Kitchen products: 15-18%. Electronics: 20-25%. Apparel: 30-35%. Her 5% assumption was fantasy
2. **Amazon doesn't fully reimburse return costs:** You pay return processing fees, damaged goods aren't always refunded, shipping isn't covered by Amazon
3. **Product quality doesn't guarantee low returns:** Shipping damage, customer expectations misalignment, and "return window" abuse account for 70% of returns
4. **Design background doesn't prevent supply chain failures:** Her manufacturer's QC varied batch-to-batch, causing defect rates she couldn't prevent

## The Math
- Revenue (7 months): $12,500
- Direct costs (COGS): $4,200
- FBA fees, referral, storage: $3,100
- Returns + processing losses: $3,400
- Time (190 hours @ $15/hr opportunity cost): $2,850
- Inventory liquidation loss: $700
- Total loss: **-$3,650** (rounded to $3,600)
- Effective hourly rate: **-$19.21/hour**

**Lesson:** "I thought my product quality would result in lower returns. I was arrogant. Returns aren't about quality—they're about shipping damage, customer expectations, and the abuse of Amazon's generous return policy. The math doesn't work at 15%+ return rates, and that's industry standard."
